The Global Policy Layer needs an owner even when it contains almost nothing. That is the easiest part of this chapter to misunderstand. Ownership of a near-empty layer feels like a job description with no work attached. The work is real, just invisible — and without it, the layer never fills in correctly.

This chapter is about that role. Who holds it, what decisions they actually make, and how the role changes shape as the layer accumulates.

### Why an owner is needed before there is anything to own

The policy layer fills in through a specific mechanism: patterns surface from the project layer (Chapter 15), candidate patterns get watched, and when conditions warrant promotion (Chapter 16), the right form — rule or standing function — gets stood up under an appropriate owner.

Every step of that mechanism requires someone whose responsibility it is to make it happen. Patterns surface only if someone is watching. Candidates get tracked only if someone holds the list. Promotion happens only if someone has the standing to declare that the four conditions are met. Without an owner, all three steps quietly fail to happen, and the policy layer stays empty not because the organization has nothing to learn but because nobody is watching the learning.

This is why the framework places the policy layer's owner from the start. The role is light at the beginning. Most of the time, there is nothing to do but listen. That is exactly what the role calls for.

### Who holds the role

The policy layer's primary owner is the AI Enablement Lead. This is the same role that coordinates across the project layer and partners with the strategic loop above. Centralizing ownership here keeps the three layers connected through a single coherent perspective — the Lead sees what the projects are revealing and is positioned to recognize when something has crossed from project-level concern to organization-level pattern.

The Lead does not own the layer alone. Once a specific policy or standing function emerges, it gets absorbed into the relevant existing function. A data hygiene program gets absorbed into the data organization. A learning function gets absorbed into the existing L&D team — added to or evolving alongside what they already do, not standing apart from it. A governance protocol gets absorbed into the relevant compliance or risk function. The Lead is the orchestrator. The function owners are the operators of what the layer eventually contains.

The executive sponsor sits behind the Lead. The sponsor's job is to provide the institutional standing the Lead may not have on their own — particularly in the early period, when the policy layer's value is mostly potential rather than visible. A patterns-surfaced-not-yet-acted-on layer can look like inactivity to people outside the framework. The sponsor is the cover that lets the Lead hold the patient stance the framework requires.

### What decisions the owner actually makes

The Lead's decision rights are deliberately narrow. Three categories.

*Tracking.* The Lead decides which surfaced findings get tracked as candidate patterns. Many findings will be project-specific and stay project-specific. Some will look like patterns and prove not to be. The Lead's first decision is what to put on the candidate list and what to leave off it. This is mostly judgment, refined over time.

*Promotion.* The Lead decides when a candidate pattern is ready for promotion against the four conditions in Chapter 16. The decision is consequential but not unilateral — promotion produces a rule or a standing function, both of which require a function owner to actually run them. The Lead recommends; the function owner commits. If no function owner is willing or able, promotion stalls and the pattern goes back to candidate status. That is a feature.

*Retirement.* The Lead decides when a candidate pattern has stopped recurring or become irrelevant. Patterns sometimes resolve themselves — the underlying issue gets addressed by a project's own work, or the organizational conditions that produced the pattern shift. A candidate-pattern list that grows monotonically is not being maintained. The Lead retires what no longer applies.

What the Lead does not do: write policy unilaterally, override function owners on the design of standing functions, or pre-approve projects on the basis of evolving policy. The first is a separation of concerns; the second is a respect for craft; the third is a governance failure mode the framework deliberately avoids.

### How the role evolves

Early on, the work is mostly watching. The candidate-pattern list is short. Promotions are rare. The Lead's main job is keeping the question *is this familiar?* live in every conversation about projects, and resisting the pressure to look productive by promoting patterns prematurely.

In the middle period, the work shifts toward orchestration. The Lead begins identifying function owners for emerging patterns. Conversations open with the existing data team about what a data hygiene program would actually look like, with the existing L&D team about how a sustained learning function would sit alongside their existing work, with the relevant risk or governance function about what a consent-review protocol would entail. These conversations precede formal promotion. They are how the framework discovers whether the four conditions can actually be met.

Later, with a populated layer, the work shifts again. The Lead now holds the boundary between project-level concerns and standing ones, and watches for when existing policies need revision or retirement. Standing functions stood up under earlier patterns may need adjustment as the organization changes. Some rules that made sense for a particular phase become obstructive once the organization has moved past it. A mature policy layer requires active stewardship, not just active accumulation.

The role does not get bigger, exactly — it gets denser. The number of active concerns grows; the principles that guide handling them stay the same.

### Examples of policies that might emerge

What the policy layer ends up containing varies widely by industry, business model, and stage. The examples below are illustrative — concrete sketches of what emergent policy looks like when it crystallizes from project experience, not prescriptions for what any particular organization should adopt. They are also not exhaustive.

- **Data — a standing function.** Project after project hits the same wall trying to access training data: legal review starts from scratch, consent gets re-checked, pipelines get rebuilt that other projects already built. A data-readiness program pre-clears common data sources and tracks what consent allows, so new projects don't pay the same toll over and over.

- **People — a new role.** As more projects build AI agents, the work of running them — monitoring, debugging, keeping them in shape — keeps falling on a couple of engineers who already have full-time jobs. The organization creates a new full-time role — Agent Orchestrator — to take on this work as the project portfolio grows.

- **Risk — a rule.** Different projects keep asking the same question — when can we move from human-in-the-loop to human-on-the-loop? — and writing their own answers from scratch. A standing protocol sets the conditions for moving between supervision levels, so projects apply the rule rather than re-invent it.

- **Culture — a hybrid.** Across projects, the same question keeps coming up: what kinds of customer-facing AI behavior will we ship, and what won't we, even if it works? A stated organizational position answers the clear cases, and a small review group handles the gray ones.

- **Governance — a rule.** Project leads keep getting stuck on the same question — which calls can they make themselves, which need a sponsor's sign-off, which need executive review — and different projects answer it differently, slowing things down. A standing rule defines who decides what, so the question gets answered once and applied everywhere.

What the examples have in common is the mechanism, not the content. Each came out of recurrence rather than foresight, has a real owner positioned to operate it, and is shaped by the specific evidence the organization's projects produced. A different organization running this framework would produce policies that look meaningfully different — even within the same dimensions.

### What this layer expects of its owner

Patience is the hardest part. The framework's policy layer rewards waiting and punishes hurrying. An owner who needs to be visibly accomplishing things will promote prematurely. An owner who tolerates long quiet periods will let the layer fill in correctly.

### What Part IV has established

Part IV closes here. The Global Policy Layer is the soil the framework sits in — slow to enrich, quietly supporting everything that grows above it, the place where what the organization keeps re-encountering at the project level eventually becomes part of how the organization is structured. It is built out of project evidence, not theoretical foresight. It has an owner from the start, even when it is mostly empty. Its shape is not predictable in advance.
