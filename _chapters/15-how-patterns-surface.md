The Global Policy Layer does not start full. At the start of an AI adoption effort it is mostly empty, and that is the correct state. Policy in this framework is not handed down from above; it is built up from below, out of the recurring evidence the project layer produces. The soil enriches from what has already grown. This chapter is about the mechanism that makes that build-up possible.

The mechanism has two halves: the surfacing of patterns from projects, and the discipline of recognizing them as patterns rather than as one-off findings. Both halves matter, and neither is automatic.

### Project findings versus systemic patterns

Every project produces findings. Most of those findings are project-specific. The data quality issue this team encountered in their support ticket archive. The cultural envelope this team sketched for their particular pilot. The governance bottleneck this team hit on this particular decision. These are useful inside the project — they sharpen the canvas, they inform the next phase of work, they explain whatever the project is going to graduate or shut down with. They are not, in themselves, candidates for organizational policy.

Some findings are different. They look the same as the project-specific kind at first glance. The difference is that they keep showing up — across projects, across teams, across time. The data access bottleneck that appeared in the support automation pilot also appears in the pricing optimization pilot. The cultural envelope question that came up in the customer service project comes up again, in similar form, in the marketing personalization project. The governance bottleneck that slowed down one project is slowing down the next.

That recurrence is the signal. A finding that shows up once is something the project has to handle. A finding that shows up across multiple projects is no longer about any individual project. It is about the organization itself. It is a pattern.

### What kinds of patterns surface

The project canvas, with its readiness, roadmap, and risk sections, is the structure that makes recurrence legible. Readiness patterns are most common — the same data access process nobody can clear quickly, the same incentive alignment gap, the same governance pathway at the wrong altitude. Roadmap patterns surface more slowly — graduation criteria too soft to enforce across multiple projects, recurring difficulty transitioning KPIs from experiential to operational. Risk patterns are the most strategically important — the same data consent question, the same ethical stance, the same autonomy threshold. When several projects independently struggle with or settle on the same answer, the organization has, without quite noticing, exposed something about itself.

### Patterns do not surface on their own

This is the most important point in the chapter. Recurrence happens whether or not anyone notices. Pattern-recognition does not.

Without an explicit watcher, the same data access bottleneck shows up in three projects, three different teams solve it three different ways, and three different lessons get filed in three different places. The organization gains nothing. The pattern was right there. Nobody was watching for it.

This is why the portfolio review described in Chapter 14 has, as one of its named functions, the surfacing of patterns for the policy layer. It is also why The Forge, introduced briefly in the executive summary and detailed in Chapter 9, exists — it is a venue where projects report in to people whose job is partly to listen for what is showing up across them. Patterns surface only when someone is making them visible.

The role here is not exotic. It is mostly a matter of holding the question "is this familiar?" in mind every time a project reports on something. When the answer is "yes, three projects in a row have hit this," the pattern has surfaced. When the answer is "no, this looks new," the finding stays project-specific.

### When does something become a pattern

The framework deliberately resists rushing to codify. A finding that has appeared once is a project finding. A finding that has appeared twice is suggestive. A finding that has appeared three or more times, in similar form across different projects, qualifies as a pattern. The threshold is not exact — the directionality of the recurrence matters more than the count. Three projects hitting the same governance bottleneck in different ways is more compelling than three projects encountering different data quality issues that look superficially similar. The discipline is reading the recurrence carefully, not just counting it.

### The filter against false patterns

Not every recurrence is real. Some is coincidental — two projects happened to hit the same surface issue for unrelated reasons. Some is artifactual — two projects appear similar because the team described them similarly. Some is local — a single business unit's problem dressed up as an organizational one. The discipline is to ask why something is recurring, not just to count that it has. The question worth asking is "have we seen this *because of something the organization is*?"

### From surfaced to promoted

A pattern that has surfaced is not yet policy. It is a candidate. It warrants consideration, not enshrinement. The next chapter takes up the question of what triggers the promotion of a candidate pattern into actual policy — what crosses the threshold from "this is a real pattern" to "this is now an organization-wide rule or a standing function."

This is where the policy layer starts filling in.
